# Twitter Thread Writing

## What This Format Is

A thread is a sequence of self-contained tweets that together develop a single idea with more depth than a single tweet allows. The algorithm rewards threads that keep readers expanding the thread to read more — dwell time and "show more" taps are the key signals. This means each tweet must earn the next one.

The thread format works for: frameworks, counterintuitive takes, step-by-step processes, story arcs, and listicles. It does NOT work for nuanced arguments, heavily caveated analysis, or any idea that requires unbroken prose to make sense.

---

## Structure

### Tweet 1: The Hook (the only tweet most people read)

**Job:** Stop the scroll and create a reason to expand.

**Patterns that work:**
- Counterintuitive claim: "Most founders waste their best lead with the first email."
- Specific outcome: "I closed a $40k contract with a 6-line cold email. Here's the exact template:"
- Provocative question: "What if your pricing model is the actual reason you lose deals?"
- Number promise: "10 things I wish I knew about cold outreach before I wasted 6 months on it:"

**Length:** 140-220 characters. Leave room for "(thread 🧵)" or "1/" — takes 8-12 chars.

**What kills the hook:** Starting with "I've been thinking about..." or "Hot take:" or restating what everyone already knows.

---

### Tweets 2-N: The Body (atomic idea escalation)

Each tweet in the body should:
1. Stand alone as a complete thought (if someone screenshots it, it should make sense)
2. Be a step up from the previous tweet — increasing insight, specificity, or surprise
3. NOT be a continuation of the previous sentence

**Escalation patterns:**
- Framework → example → implication → counterexample
- Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3 (process threads)
- Principle → failure mode → fix (diagnostic threads)

**Tweet length in body:** Varies. Short punchy tweets (under 100 chars) create rhythm. Longer tweets (200-280 chars) slow down for important points. Mix them.

**Thread length:**
- 3-5 tweets: tight, high-shareability, good for frameworks
- 6-10 tweets: standard, good for step-by-step content
- 11-20 tweets: long-form, risk of drop-off after tweet 7 — use only if every tweet earns the next
- 20+: only for historically significant, comprehensive content. Most "threads" this long are padding.

---

### Last Content Tweet: The Punchline

The last content tweet before the CTA should be the strongest single insight in the thread — the thing readers will save or screenshot. Don't end with a summary. End with a hit.

---

### CTA Tweet: The Close

**Placement:** Always the last tweet.

**CTA options (ranked by performance):**
1. Follow for more like this (low friction, scales audience)
2. Save this for when you need it (boosts algorithmic reach via saves)
3. RT the first tweet if this was useful (extends reach)
4. Link to related content / product (higher friction, more valuable per click)

**What to avoid:** Multiple CTAs in the last tweet. "Follow me, RT this, and check out my course" competes with itself.

---

## Rules

- Hook tweet is written last — after you know what the thread delivers.
- Every tweet stands alone if screenshot.
- No "continued from above" or partial sentences that bleed into the next tweet.
- Don't number tweets (1/12, 2/12) unless the sequence order is critical — numbering signals low confidence that readers will stay.
- Avoid "Anyway," "To summarize," or "In conclusion" tweets — just state the conclusion.
- Thread length = number of distinct insights, not word count targets.

## Platform Notes (as of 2025)

- Saves count more than likes for algorithmic distribution.
- Replies from the author inside the thread (adding context) can boost visibility.
- Long threads get "Show more" prompts — this is a feature, not a bug; it means readers are engaged.
- Images in threads significantly increase click-through on the tweet they accompany.
