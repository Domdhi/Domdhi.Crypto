# Newsletter Writing

## What This Format Is

A newsletter is a recurring relationship document. Unlike articles (searchable, evergreen) or social posts (ephemeral), newsletters arrive in an inbox the reader chose to give you — they have higher intimacy and higher commitment than any other content channel. The job of each issue is to reward that intimacy: deliver enough value that the reader doesn't regret subscribing.

The rules here apply to regular-cadence newsletters (weekly, biweekly, monthly). One-off announcements are a different format.

---

## Structure

### 1. Subject Line (the only thing that determines if the email gets opened)

**Length:** 40-50 characters for mobile preview
**Pattern options:**
- Question: "Is your pricing model killing your close rate?"
- Specific outcome: "How I cut my reporting time from 4 hours to 20 minutes"
- Number: "3 things that make proposals actually close"
- Intrigue: "The objection I've never heard a good answer to"

**What to avoid:** Subject lines that summarize the content. If they know what's in it, they don't open it. The subject line creates a question; the email answers it.

**Preview text:** The 80-100 character snippet after the subject line. Treat it as a second subject line, not a repeat of the first.

---

### 2. Hook (first 2-3 sentences)

The hook earns the read. It should do one of these:
- State a non-obvious observation
- Open a loop the reader wants closed
- Hit a nerve the reader has felt but not articulated

**Good:** "Every time I lose a deal, I ask the prospect why. Nine times out of ten, they tell me the problem was in the discovery call — not the proposal."

**Bad:** "Welcome to this week's newsletter. This week we're talking about sales."

No setup. No "hi, here's what's in this issue." Start with the thing.

---

### 3. Body (the payoff)

**Length by cadence:**
- Daily: 150-300 words. One idea, clearly.
- Weekly: 400-800 words. One main topic, 1-2 supporting points.
- Monthly: 800-1,500 words. Multiple sections with clear visual breaks.

**Structure options:**
- Single-thread: one idea developed fully
- Listicle-hybrid: narrative intro → 3-5 bullet insights → narrative close
- Before/after: what I thought → what happened → what I know now

**Visual rhythm:** Headers or bold section labels every 150-200 words. Readers scan before they commit. Make scanning reward the reader, not punish them.

---

### 4. CTA (one, at the end)

One CTA per issue. Not "reply if you found this useful AND share with a friend AND check out my new product."

CTA types ranked by friction:
1. Reply (lowest friction — "Hit reply and tell me...")
2. Click to read more
3. Share with one person
4. Buy / sign up / book

Match CTA friction to relationship depth. Early subscribers get low-friction CTAs. Engaged long-term readers can handle ask CTAs.

---

## Rules

- One topic per issue. If you have two ideas, you have two newsletters.
- Subject line never summarizes — it creates a question.
- Hook never starts with "In this issue..." or "Welcome to..."
- No more than one CTA.
- Preview text is a second subject line, not a repeat.
- Unsubscribe link is non-negotiable and easy to find.

## Voice Calibration

This skill teaches structure. Voice comes from the writer agent (shadow or ghostwriting). Load both when writing voice-matched newsletter content:
- This skill: structure, hook discipline, CTA placement
- ghostwriting skill: voice rules, anti-patterns, approved phrasings
